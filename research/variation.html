Variation-aware performance and power analysis

What and why

Manufacturing variations in semiconductor devices introduce unpredictability in how these circuits behave by making key design parameters such as delays, power consumption, error tolerance and lifetime increasingly unpredictable. This has made microelectronic system design more challenging at all levels, as the traditional guardbands to hide such variations have vastly exceeded design flexibility available in circuit-level implementations. We need better models and methods to accurately account for the effect of well-characterized variability mechanisms and use these at RT- and system-level designs where much greater flexibility exists to restructure designs to mitigate these effects. Traditional design verification only checks for functional correctness. It does not check for performance/power properties at RT-level. Instead of being variation-agnostic, if the RTL design is aware of the variations at the lower level, it can generate early reliable estimates of performance and power. This can drastically reduce the overhead of changing a design decision after reaching the gate/circuit level. How Our group has developed SHARPE, a tool suite that can give formal statistical guarantees on RTL designs. Given an input probability distribution and an RTL design, the tool computes signal probabilities through static analysis of the RTL design. It uses probabilistic model checking to check the property in the RTL Markov model. We have developed compositional methods to scale probabilistic model checking for their purpose. The tool also has a statistical model checking option in RTL. As the number of simulation samples increase, the confidence in the property becomes higher. This is a more scalable, semi-formal method to give statistical guarantees. We have also developed methods to accelerate statistical model checking for multiple failure regions using clustering and Variational Bayesian inference. So far, SHARPE has been used for providing guarantees on performance for better-than-worst-case designs. We have also used SHARPE for analyzing NBTI ageing effects at RTL and to verify dynamic power management schemes.

And so what…

The SHARPE solution for analyzing NBTI effects early in the design cycle was developed as part of a collaboration withTexas Instruments. TI circuits that are vulnerable to NBTI effects were detected at the transistor level, which caused a significant delay and resource wastage when the design decisions needed to be revised at the higher levels. Prof. Vasudevan’s student, Jayanand Asok Kumar won the silver medal at the ACM SRC (student research competition) in DAC 2011 for the NBTI solution. Prof Vasudevan and her students are actively engaged with the researchers in the NSF Expeditions program for variability. The SHARPE project has researched techniques that will improve predictability of design parameters, and build a new generation of variability-aware design optimization and synthesis tools.

What next?

Power is critically important to modern systems. We want to analyze and estimate power at the higher levels of design. This can be used for power optimizations as well as for providing power budgets and contracts to the software applications and algorithms that rely on these estimates. We would like to models from experimental data that provide increased observability of variability effects in RT- and system-level designs and new verification and design optimization techniques that enable efficient quantification and checking of variability affected design properties.
